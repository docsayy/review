Why Do Patients Need Intubation?

The four major indications for intubation are:

  • Acute hypoxemic respiratory failure
  • Acute hypercapnic respiratory failure
  • Airway protection
  • Severe acidemia

Hypoxemia vs Hypoxia

Hypoxemia refers to low oxygen levels in the blood.

Hypoxia refers to inadequate oxygen delivery to tissues resulting in organ dysfunction.

Hypoxia is generally more dangerous because tissue injury is already occurring.

Ventilator Modes

Assist-Control (AC)

The ventilator guarantees a minimum respiratory rate while also assisting patient-triggered breaths.

Volume Control

  • Fixed tidal volume
  • Variable pressure
  • Reliable ventilation

Pressure Control

  • Fixed pressure
  • Variable tidal volume
  • Better pressure limitation

Ventilation vs Oxygenation

Ventilation (CO₂ Removal)

  • Tidal Volume ↑
  • Respiratory Rate ↑

Oxygenation (O₂ Delivery)

  • PEEP ↑
  • FiO₂ ↑
Remember: CO₂ problems → adjust RR and TV. Oxygen problems → adjust PEEP and FiO₂.

P/F Ratio & ARDS

P/F Ratio = PaO₂ ÷ FiO₂

ARDS Severity

Severity P/F Ratio
Mild < 300
Moderate < 200
Severe < 100

Ventilator Pressures

Parameter Goal
Peak Pressure < 40–45
Plateau Pressure < 30
Driving Pressure < 15
Driving Pressure = Plateau Pressure − PEEP

Why PEEP Matters

PEEP keeps alveoli open at end-expiration and prevents alveolar collapse.

  • Improves oxygenation
  • Prevents atelectasis
  • Reduces alveolar derecruitment
  • Improves gas exchange

Landmark Trials

ARDSNet Trial

  • 6 mL/kg vs 12 mL/kg tidal volume
  • Low tidal volume improved survival
  • Current standard = 6 mL/kg predicted body weight

PROSEVA Trial

  • Proning improved survival
  • Consider when P/F < 150

ACURASYS Trial

  • Neuromuscular blockade improved outcomes in severe ARDS

Weaning & Extubation

Goals

  • FiO₂ < 60%
  • Gradually reduce PEEP
  • Daily spontaneous breathing trials
  • Daily sedation vacations

RSBI

RSBI = Respiratory Rate ÷ Tidal Volume (Liters)

RSBI < 105 predicts successful extubation.

Cuff Leak Test

A positive cuff leak suggests minimal airway edema and safer extubation.

When to Consider Tracheostomy

Patients requiring prolonged mechanical ventilation should be evaluated for tracheostomy around day 10.

  • Improved comfort
  • Less sedation
  • Easier secretion management
  • Reduced tracheal injury

ICU Ventilator Pearls

Remember:
  • TV + RR = Ventilation
  • PEEP + FiO₂ = Oxygenation
  • P/F ratio determines ARDS severity
  • Plateau Pressure < 30
  • Driving Pressure < 15
  • Low TV ventilation saves lives
  • Prone when P/F < 150
  • RSBI < 105 predicts extubation success
